Module 7: Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo

Noli Me Tangere (1887)

Historical Background and Writing

Noli Me Tangere was conceived and written during Rizal's stay in Europe. The idea came to him after reading Harriet Beecher Stowe's Uncle Tom's Cabin. He began writing the novel in Madrid and completed it in Berlin in 1887. The publication was funded by his friend Máximo Viola, costing 300 pesos for 2,000 copies.

Title and Significance

The title "Noli Me Tangere" (Touch Me Not) has multiple meanings:

  • Biblical reference to Christ's words to Mary Magdalene
  • Medical term for cancer in its advanced stage
  • Metaphor for the social cancer afflicting Philippine society

Plot Summary

The story follows Juan Crisóstomo Ibarra, returning to the Philippines after studying in Europe. His idealistic plans for reform, particularly in education, are thwarted by corrupt Spanish friars. The novel interweaves multiple narratives:

  • Ibarra's love story with María Clara
  • The tragic tale of Sisa and her sons
  • Elías's revolutionary perspective
  • The corruption of the church and government

CBCP Analysis

The Catholic Bishops Conference of the Philippines analyzed the novel's 333 pages:

  • 25 pages - Nationalistic content
  • 120 pages - State of the country
  • 170 pages - Content against Catholic faith
  • 18 pages - Other content

Key Characters

Each character represents different aspects of Filipino society:

  • Juan Crisóstomo Ibarra - An educated Filipino seeking reform
  • María Clara - Symbol of the colonized Philippines
  • Padre Dámaso - Represents abusive Spanish friars
  • Elías - Voice of the oppressed masses
  • Tasio - The enlightened philosopher
  • Sisa - Represents suffering mothers

El Filibusterismo (1891)

Historical Context and Development

Written between 1887 and 1891, El Filibusterismo emerged from Rizal's growing disillusionment with the Spanish colonial system. The novel was published in Ghent, Belgium, with financial assistance from Valentín Ventura. Unlike its predecessor, this sequel took a darker, more revolutionary tone, reflecting the changing political climate in the Philippines.

Complete Plot Analysis

Set thirteen years after the events of Noli Me Tangere, the story follows Crisóstomo Ibarra, now transformed into the wealthy jeweler Simoun. Key plot elements include:

  • Simoun's elaborate revenge plot using his influence with the Governor-General
  • The student movement for educational reform
  • Basilio's struggle between loyalty and conscience
  • The failed lamp plot at the wedding feast
  • Simoun's final confession and death at Padre Florentino's house

Character Evolution

The novel shows significant character development:

  • Ibarra/Simoun: From idealistic reformer to cynical revolutionary
  • Basilio: From orphaned boy to medical student facing moral choices
  • Isagani: Representing the idealistic youth movement
  • Padre Florentino: The voice of moral wisdom and hope
  • Juli: Symbol of innocent victims of social injustice

Themes and Social Commentary

The novel explores deeper themes than its predecessor:

  • The moral cost of revenge and violence
  • Education as a tool for social change
  • The role of youth in national reform
  • The cycle of oppression and resistance
  • The question of means versus ends in achieving freedom

Symbolism and Literary Devices

Key symbols include:

  • The jewels: Representing the deceptive nature of wealth and power
  • The lamp: Symbol of enlightenment turned to destruction
  • The wedding feast: Metaphor for Philippine society
  • The sea: Representing cleansing and renewal

Impact and Legacy

Historical Significance

Both novels had profound effects on Philippine society:

  • Inspired the Philippine Revolution of 1896
  • Contributed to the growth of Filipino nationalism
  • Led to significant reforms in colonial administration
  • Established Filipino literature on the world stage

Modern Relevance

The novels continue to influence contemporary Philippines:

  • Required reading in schools through Republic Act 1425
  • Source material for various artistic adaptations
  • Reference point for discussions of social justice
  • Inspiration for modern Filipino literature

Educational Value

The novels serve multiple educational purposes:

  • Historical documentation of 19th century Philippines
  • Literary analysis of social reform themes
  • Study of character development and symbolism
  • Understanding of Philippine nationalism
